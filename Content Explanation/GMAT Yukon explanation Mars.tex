\section*{Analysis of \texttt{Ex\_Yukon\_MarsLaunchWindowAnalysis}}

This GMAT script utilizes a \textbf{Multiple Shooting} strategy to find a valid interplanetary trajectory. Rather than guessing an initial Earth-departure velocity that magically hits Mars (which is nearly impossible due to numerical sensitivity), we use an optimizer to "stitch" together two trajectories.

\subsection*{Key Algorithmic Components}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Dual Propagation:} The script creates \texttt{satForward} (propagated from Earth) and \texttt{satBackward} (propagated from Mars). They meet at a temporal \texttt{patchEpoch}.
    \item \textbf{NonLinearConstraint (The Bridge):} The solver iterates until the position ($X,Y,Z$) and velocity ($V_X, V_Y, V_Z$) of both spacecraft are identical at the patch point. 
    \begin{lstlisting}[language=C++]
NonlinearConstraint Yukon1(satForward.SunMJ2000Ec.X = satBackward.SunMJ2000Ec.X);
NonlinearConstraint Yukon1(satForward.SunMJ2000Ec.VX = satBackward.SunMJ2000Ec.VX);
    \end{lstlisting}
    \item \textbf{The Cost Function:} \texttt{cost = sqrt(satForward.EarthSunRot.VX\^2 + ...)}
    This calculates the velocity discontinuity. The optimizer's goal is to drive this cost to zero (perfect continuity) while minimizing the total energy required.
\end{itemize}
