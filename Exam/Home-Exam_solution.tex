\section*{Solution 1: Perturbative Forces (20 Points)}

\textbf{(a) Analytical Derivation of $J_2$} \\
The disturbing potential function for the Earth's oblateness ($J_2$) is given by:
\begin{equation}
    R_{J2} = \frac{3}{2} \frac{\mu J_2 R_\oplus^2}{r^3} \left( \frac{z^2}{r^2} - \frac{1}{3} \right)
\end{equation}
Taking the gradient of the potential ($\nabla R_{J2}$), we obtain the vector acceleration $\boldsymbol{a}_{J2}$ in the ECI frame:
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{a}_{J2} = -\frac{3}{2} J_2 \left(\frac{\mu}{r^2}\right) \left(\frac{R_\oplus}{r}\right)^2 
    \begin{bmatrix} 
        \left(1 - 5\frac{z^2}{r^2}\right)\frac{x}{r} \\ 
        \left(1 - 5\frac{z^2}{r^2}\right)\frac{y}{r} \\ 
        \left(3 - 5\frac{z^2}{r^2}\right)\frac{z}{r} 
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}
\textit{Primary Effects:} The equatorial bulge creates a non-central gravitational pull. This torque causes the orbital plane to precess, leading to the secular drift of the Right Ascension of the Ascending Node ($\Omega$), known as nodal regression. Furthermore, the non-spherical potential alters the radial force balance, causing the Argument of Perigee ($\omega$) to rotate over time (apsidal rotation).

\textbf{(b) Python Simulation \& CAM} \\
The student should provide a Python script utilizing `scipy.integrate.solve_ivp` with the $\boldsymbol{a}_{J2}$ acceleration added to the 2-body equations of motion.

\textbf{Chief Architect's Memo (CAM) - $J_2$ Discrepancies:} 
\textit{"The analytical equation for nodal regression ($\dot{\Omega} = -\frac{3}{2} n J_2 (R_\oplus/p)^2 \cos i$) assumes constant semi-major axis ($a$) and eccentricity ($e$). However, in the numerical simulation, the inclusion of atmospheric drag constantly reduces the semi-major axis, thus decreasing the orbital period ($n$) and the parameter ($p$). Consequently, the numerical drift rate of $\Omega$ accelerates over the 30-day period compared to the constant analytical prediction, showcasing the cross-coupling of perturbative forces."}

\section*{Solution 2: Interplanetary Mission to Venus (25 Points)}

\textbf{(a) Analytical Hohmann Transfer} \\
Assuming $r_\oplus = 149.6 \times 10^6 \text{ km}$, $r_v = 108.2 \times 10^6 \text{ km}$, and $\mu_\odot = 1.327 \times 10^{11} \text{ km}^3/\text{s}^2$.
\begin{align*}
    a_{tx} &= \frac{r_\oplus + r_v}{2} = 128.9 \times 10^6 \text{ km} \\
    v_{tx,1} &= \sqrt{\mu_\odot \left( \frac{2}{r_\oplus} - \frac{1}{a_{tx}} \right)} = 27.27 \text{ km/s} \\
    v_\oplus &= \sqrt{\frac{\mu_\odot}{r_\oplus}} = 29.78 \text{ km/s}
\end{align*}
Because Venus is an inner planet, the spacecraft must slow down relative to Earth. 
\begin{align*}
    v_\infty &= v_{tx,1} - v_\oplus = 27.27 - 29.78 = -2.51 \text{ km/s} \quad \text{(Trailing edge departure)} \\
    C_3 &= v_\infty^2 = (-2.51)^2 = \mathbf{6.30 \text{ km}^2/\text{s}^2}
\end{align*}

\textbf{(b \& c) Porkchop Plot \& GMAT CAM} \\
Students should use Python (e.g., `poliastro.plotting.porkchop`) to find the exact 2028/2029 launch window. Because planetary orbits are slightly eccentric and inclined, the actual $C_3$ will be slightly higher (e.g., $\sim 8 - 12 \text{ km}^2/\text{s}^2$) than the coplanar assumption. 

\textbf{Chief Architect's Memo (CAM) - GMAT Venus Targeting:}
\textit{"In GMAT, the differential corrector was set to target the required $C_3$ energy upon exiting Earth's sphere of influence (SOI). Since Venus is an inner planet, the optimal departure burn was applied at the periapsis of the parking orbit aligned such that the departure hyperbola exited opposite to Earth's velocity vector (trailing edge). B-plane parameters at Venus (BdotT, BdotR) were targeted to ensure planetary capture rather than a flyby."}

\section*{Solution 3: Deep Space Gravity Assist - Earth-Jupiter-Saturn (35 Points)}

\textbf{(a \& b) Vector Geometry \& Flyby Parameters} \\
Let $V_J$ be Jupiter's heliocentric velocity. The spacecraft arrives with relative velocity $\boldsymbol{v}_\infty^-$ and departs with $\boldsymbol{v}_\infty^+$.
\begin{align*}
    \boldsymbol{v}_{arr} &= \boldsymbol{V}_J + \boldsymbol{v}_\infty^- \\
    \boldsymbol{v}_{dep} &= \boldsymbol{V}_J + \boldsymbol{v}_\infty^+
\end{align*}
The gravity assist relies on rotating the $\boldsymbol{v}_\infty$ vector by turning angle $\delta$ without changing its magnitude ($|\boldsymbol{v}_\infty^-| = |\boldsymbol{v}_\infty^+| = v_\infty$).
\begin{equation}
    \sin\left(\frac{\delta}{2}\right) = \frac{1}{1 + \frac{r_p v_\infty^2}{\mu_J}} \implies r_p = \frac{\mu_J}{v_\infty^2} \left( \frac{1}{\sin(\delta/2)} - 1 \right)
\end{equation}

\textbf{Chief Architect's Memo (CAM) - Flyby Safety:}
\textit{"Based on the calculated $r_p$ of roughly $4.5 \times 10^5 \text{ km}$, the flyby altitude is well above Jupiter's equatorial radius ($71,492 \text{ km}$). The trajectory does not intersect the atmosphere. However, it passes deeply through the Jovian radiation belts (which extend intensely up to $3 \times 10^5 \text{ km}$). While kinetically safe, the spacecraft avionics will require heavy radiation shielding to survive this turning angle, which impacts our mass budget."}

\textbf{(c) GMAT Expected Setup} \\
The student's script must define `JupiterBPlane` and target `Sat.JupiterBPlane.BdotT` and `BdotR`. The total $\Delta V$ savings is found by subtracting the E-J Hohmann departure from the E-S Hohmann departure ($\approx 10.5 \text{ km/s} - 8.8 \text{ km/s} = 1.7 \text{ km/s}$ saved at LEO).

\section*{Solution 4: Low-Thrust Lunar Transfer Analysis (20 Points)}

\textbf{(a) Analytical Spiral Calculation} \\
Using the continuous thrust equations to evaluate a spiral from $h_0 = 500 \text{ km}$ to lunar SOI ($r_f \approx 384,400 \text{ km}$). 
\begin{align*}
    v_0 &= \sqrt{\frac{\mu}{R_\oplus + 500}} \approx 7.61 \text{ km/s} \\
    v_f &= \sqrt{\frac{\mu}{r_f}} \approx 1.01 \text{ km/s} \\
    \Delta V &\approx |v_f - v_0| = 6.60 \text{ km/s}
\end{align*}
Using the ideal rocket equation with exhaust velocity $v_{ex} = I_{sp} g_0 = 2000 \times 9.81 = 19,620 \text{ m/s} = 19.62 \text{ km/s}$:
\begin{equation}
    m_f = m_0 e^{-\Delta V / v_{ex}} = 1500 e^{-6.60 / 19.62} \approx 1071.7 \text{ kg}
\end{equation}
Propellant consumed: $m_p = 1500 - 1071.7 = \mathbf{428.3 \text{ kg}}$.
Time of flight:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta t = \frac{m_p v_{ex}}{T} = \frac{428.3 \times 19,620}{0.2} \approx 42.0 \times 10^6 \text{ seconds} \approx \mathbf{486 \text{ days}}
\end{equation}

\textbf{(b) Comparative Trade Study (CAM)} \\
\textbf{Chief Architect's Memo (CAM) - Low-Thrust vs. Chemical Transfer:}
\textit{"The Solar Electric Propulsion (SEP) architecture provides a massive advantage in payload mass fraction. Consuming only $428 \text{ kg}$ of propellant, we can deliver over $1,000 \text{ kg}$ of dry mass to the Moon. A traditional chemical transfer ($I_{sp} \approx 315 \text{ s}$) requires a $\Delta V$ of roughly $3.1 \text{ km/s}$ (TLI impulsive), requiring over $950 \text{ kg}$ of propellant, leaving drastically less room for scientific payload. }

\textit{However, the operational cost of SEP is severe. The time of flight stretches to over 16 months compared to the 3-5 days of a chemical transfer. During this extended spiral, the spacecraft spends weeks slowly traversing the Van Allen radiation belts, risking severe degradation to its solar arrays and unshielded electronics. Mission operations also become complex, requiring continuous thrusting algorithms and near-constant Deep Space Network (DSN) tracking, compared to a single automated TLI burn."}
