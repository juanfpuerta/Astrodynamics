\section*{Part 1: Analytical Baseline Solution (Colab / Python)}

\textbf{Assumed Planetary Constants:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Sun: $\mu_\odot = 132,712,440,018 \text{ km}^3/\text{s}^2$
    \item Earth: $\mu_\oplus = 398,600 \text{ km}^3/\text{s}^2$, $R_\oplus = 6,378 \text{ km}$, $r_1 \approx 149.6 \times 10^6 \text{ km}$
    \item LEO Parking Orbit: $h = 300 \text{ km} \rightarrow r_{park} = 6,678 \text{ km}$
    \item Mars: $r_2 \approx 227.9 \times 10^6 \text{ km}$
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Step 1.1: The Heliocentric Transfer}
Calculate the semi-major axis of the Hohmann transfer ellipse ($a_{tx}$) and the velocity required at perihelion ($V_{tx,1}$):
\begin{align*}
    a_{tx} &= \frac{r_1 + r_2}{2} = 188.75 \times 10^6 \text{ km} \\
    V_\oplus &= \sqrt{\frac{\mu_\odot}{r_1}} = 29.78 \text{ km/s} \\
    V_{tx,1} &= \sqrt{\mu_\odot \left( \frac{2}{r_1} - \frac{1}{a_{tx}} \right)} = 32.73 \text{ km/s}
\end{align*}
The required hyperbolic excess velocity ($\boldsymbol{v}_\infty$) and characteristic energy ($C_3$) are:
\begin{align*}
    v_\infty &= V_{tx,1} - V_\oplus = 32.73 - 29.78 = \textbf{2.95 km/s} \\
    C_3 &= v_\infty^2 = \textbf{8.70 km}^2/\textbf{s}^2
\end{align*}

\textbf{Step 1.2: The Geocentric Escape Burn ($\Delta V_{TMI}$)}
Calculate the velocity in the 300 km LEO parking orbit, and the required periapsis velocity of the escape hyperbola:
\begin{align*}
    V_{LEO} &= \sqrt{\frac{\mu_\oplus}{r_{park}}} = \sqrt{\frac{398600}{6678}} = 7.72 \text{ km/s} \\
    V_{p} &= \sqrt{C_3 + \frac{2\mu_\oplus}{r_{park}}} = \sqrt{8.70 + \frac{2(398600)}{6678}} = 11.31 \text{ km/s}
\end{align*}
The impulsive Trans-Mars Injection burn is:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta V_{TMI} = V_{p} - V_{LEO} = 11.31 - 7.72 = \textbf{3.59 km/s}
\end{equation}
\textit{Grading Note: Accept analytical answers between 3.55 and 3.65 km/s depending on the precision of the planetary radii/ephemeris data used by the students.}

\section*{Part 3: The Launch Window \& CAM Evaluation}

Upon shifting the \texttt{Epoch} forward by 48 hours, the students will observe that the targeter requires significantly more iterations to converge. The $\Delta V$ magnitude will increase, heavily loading the Normal and Binormal components.

\subsection*{Model Chief Architect's Memo (CAM)}
\textit{Look for the following physical justifications when grading the CAMs:}

\begin{quote}
\textbf{Student CAM:} ``The analytical Python baseline (3.59 km/s) assumed a perfectly optimal, coplanar Hohmann transfer where Earth and Mars are exactly aligned at $180^\circ$ at arrival. By shifting the launch epoch forward by 48 hours, we missed the optimal planetary phasing. 

To still intercept Mars, the trajectory can no longer be a perfectly tangent, purely prograde ellipse. The GMAT Differential Corrector was forced to increase the $\Delta V$ magnitude, specifically utilizing the out-of-plane Normal and Binormal velocity vectors, to artificially 'bend' the flight path and crank the orbital plane to catch Mars at its new location. This validates why missing a launch window incurs severe propellant penalties.''
\end{quote}

\textbf{Grading Criteria for CAM (100 pts total):}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{40 pts:} Identifies the breakdown of the $180^\circ$ Hohmann alignment assumption.
    \item \textbf{40 pts:} Explains that the extra $\Delta V$ went into out-of-plane/radial steering (changing the trajectory shape).
    \item \textbf{20 pts:} Clearly bridges the gap between analytical limits and numerical realities.
\end{itemize}
