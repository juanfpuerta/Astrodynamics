\section*{The Hyperbolic Departure Asymptote}
When we calculate an interplanetary trajectory using Patched Conics, the solution provides a required \textbf{Hyperbolic Departure Asymptote} ($\boldsymbol{v}_\infty$). This is the mathematical "straight line" the spacecraft must follow as it leaves Earth's Sphere of Influence (SOI). 

To define this asymptote in 3D geocentric space, we use three distinct parameters: Right Ascension ($\beta$), Declination ($\delta$), and Characteristic Energy ($C_3$).

\subsection*{1. Right Ascension ($\beta$): The "Compass Heading"}
Right Ascension is measured in the Earth's equatorial plane. It starts from the Vernal Equinox ($0^\circ$, defined as the +X axis) and rotates counter-clockwise.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Physical Meaning:} $\beta$ dictates the general direction the spacecraft must fly horizontally. 
    \item \textbf{Mission Impact:} Because the Earth rotates, a specific launch site will only sweep through the correct alignment for $\beta$ at a specific time of day. This drives the timing of the daily launch window.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{2. Declination ($\delta$): The "Tilt"}
Declination represents the latitude of the departure vector. It measures how far above ($+$) or below ($-$) the Earth's equator the spacecraft must fly to intercept the target planet.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Mission Impact (The Inclination Trap):} To depart along a vector with a specific declination, the spacecraft's parking orbit \textit{must} be tilted to at least that same angle. Therefore, the absolute mathematical constraint for an interplanetary parking orbit is:
    \begin{equation}
        i \ge |\delta|
    \end{equation}
    If you attempt to launch to Mars ($\delta \approx 20^\circ$) from a purely equatorial orbit ($i = 0^\circ$), an impossible out-of-plane maneuver would be required.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{3. Characteristic Energy ($C_3$): The "Muscle"}
Characteristic energy represents the raw power required to escape Earth's gravity well and reach the target planet. It is defined mathematically as the square of the hyperbolic excess velocity:
\begin{equation}
    C_3 = v_\infty^2
\end{equation}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{High $C_3$ ($> 20 \text{ km}^2/\text{s}^2$):} The spacecraft has massive kinetic energy. Earth's gravity has little time to act on it, resulting in a very flat, almost straight hyperbolic trajectory.
    \item \textbf{Low $C_3$ ($< 10 \text{ km}^2/\text{s}^2$):} The spacecraft is barely escaping. Earth's gravity heavily bends the trajectory (Gravity Drag), causing the flight path to execute a sharp "U-turn" around the planet before straightening out along the asymptote.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Aligning the Parking Orbit (RAAN)}
To successfully execute a Trans-Mars Injection (TMI), the spacecraft's parking orbit plane must perfectly slice through the $\boldsymbol{v}_\infty$ vector in 3D space. Once the inclination ($i$) is chosen, there are exactly two valid Right Ascension of the Ascending Node ($\Omega$) values that satisfy this geometry:

\begin{equation}
    \sin(\Omega - \beta) = \frac{-\tan(\delta)}{\tan(i)}
\end{equation}

By solving this equation analytically, mission designers determine the exact orbital elements required before inputting the scenario into numerical integrators like GMAT.
