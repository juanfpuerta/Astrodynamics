\section*{Laboratory Overview}
In modern space mission design, analytical equations provide the baseline, but numerical targeting tools build the actual mission. This laboratory introduces NASA's General Mission Analysis Tool (GMAT). 

For each mission scenario below, you must first calculate the theoretical impulsive maneuvers ($\Delta V$) by hand. You will then build the mission in GMAT and use the \textbf{Differential Corrector (DC)} to numerically find the exact burn requirements. Finally, you will compare the theoretical and numerical results.

\textit{Important GMAT Setup Note:} To ensure your GMAT numerical results match your 2-body analytical calculations, you must configure your Propagator (`DefaultProp`) to use a \textbf{Point Mass} gravity model for Earth. Turn off atmospheric drag, solar radiation pressure, and point mass gravity from the Moon and Sun. Use $\mu_{\oplus} = 398600.4415 \text{ km}^3/\text{s}^2$ and $R_{\oplus} = 6378.136 \text{ km}$.

\section*{Exercise 1: LEO to GEO Hohmann Transfer}
\textbf{Mission Objective:} Transfer a communications satellite from a Low Earth Orbit (LEO) parking orbit to a Geostationary Earth Orbit (GEO).

\textbf{Given Parameters:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Initial LEO Altitude: $h_1 = 300 \text{ km}$ (Circular, $i = 0^\circ$)
    \item Target GEO Altitude: $h_2 = 35,786 \text{ km}$ (Circular, $i = 0^\circ$)
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Part A: Analytical Calculation}
Calculate the following parameters by hand (show all work):
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The semi-major axis of the transfer ellipse ($a_{tx}$).
    \item The initial parking orbit velocity ($v_1$).
    \item The required periapsis velocity of the transfer orbit ($v_{tx,1}$).
    \item The First Impulsive Burn ($\Delta V_1$).
    \item The apoapsis velocity of the transfer orbit ($v_{tx,2}$).
    \item The final circular GEO velocity ($v_2$).
    \item The Second Impulsive Burn ($\Delta V_2$) to circularize the orbit.
    \item The Total Mission $\Delta V$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Part B: GMAT Simulation \& Differential Correction}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Resources Setup:} Create a Spacecraft in the 300 km LEO. Create two `ImpulsiveBurn` objects (set to `Local` and `VNB`). Ensure a `DifferentialCorrector` is available.
    \item \textbf{Mission Sequence - Burn 1:} 
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Create a \texttt{Target} sequence.
            \item \texttt{Vary} `ImpulsiveBurn1.Element1` (Prograde velocity). Use your analytical $\Delta V_1$ as the initial guess.
            \item \texttt{Maneuver} the spacecraft using `ImpulsiveBurn1`.
            \item \texttt{Achieve} a target Apoapsis radius (`Sat.Earth.RMAG = 42164`).
        \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Mission Sequence - Coast \& Burn 2:}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item \texttt{Propagate} the spacecraft to `Sat.Earth.Apoapsis`.
            \item Create a second \texttt{Target} sequence.
            \item \texttt{Vary} `ImpulsiveBurn2.Element1`. Use your analytical $\Delta V_2$ as the initial guess.
            \item \texttt{Maneuver} the spacecraft using `ImpulsiveBurn2`.
            \item \texttt{Achieve} a target eccentricity of exactly zero (`Sat.Earth.ECC = 0.0`).
        \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Deliverable:} Run the mission. Record the exact $\Delta V_1$ and $\Delta V_2$ values found by the Differential Corrector in the message window. Calculate the percentage error between your hand calculations and the GMAT numerical solution.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Exercise 2: Pure Orbital Plane Change}
\textbf{Mission Objective:} Correct a launch inclination error. A satellite was deployed into a circular orbit with an incorrect inclination and must perform an impulsive maneuver to reach an equatorial orbit.

\textbf{Given Parameters:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Orbit Altitude: $h = 1,200 \text{ km}$ (Circular)
    \item Initial Inclination: $i_1 = 28.5^\circ$ (Kennedy Space Center launch)
    \item Target Inclination: $i_2 = 0^\circ$ (Equatorial)
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Part A: Analytical Calculation}
A pure plane change is most efficient when the orbit intersects the target plane. 
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Calculate the orbital velocity ($v_c$) at this altitude.
    \item Using the vector triangle relationship for a pure plane change, calculate the required impulsive maneuver ($\Delta V$):
    \begin{equation}
        \Delta V = 2 v_c \sin \left( \frac{\Delta i}{2} \right)
    \end{equation}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Part B: GMAT Simulation \& Differential Correction}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Resources Setup:} Initialize the spacecraft at $h = 1200 \text{ km}$ and $i = 28.5^\circ$. Create an `ImpulsiveBurn` object set to the `VNB` frame. 
    \item \textbf{Mission Sequence:}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item \texttt{Propagate} the spacecraft to the descending node (`Sat.Earth.DescendingNode`). \textit{Note: Plane changes must occur at the nodes.}
            \item Create a \texttt{Target} sequence.
            \item \texttt{Vary} `ImpulsiveBurn1.Element2` (Normal velocity). Use your analytical $\Delta V$ as the initial guess. Give it a lower limit of $-5.0$ and an upper limit of $5.0$ km/s.
            \item \texttt{Maneuver} the spacecraft.
            \item \texttt{Achieve} a target inclination of zero (`Sat.Earth.INC = 0.0`).
        \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Deliverable:} Extract the Normal $\Delta V$ from the GMAT output. Does the sign of the normal burn (positive or negative) match the right-hand rule for a descending node plane change? Explain.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Exercise 3: Interplanetary Departure (Patched Conic)}
\textbf{Mission Objective:} A Mars-bound spacecraft is waiting in a Low Earth Parking orbit. The interplanetary mission design team has dictated that the spacecraft must leave Earth's Sphere of Influence (SOI) with a specific hyperbolic excess velocity ($v_\infty$). 

\textbf{Given Parameters:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Earth Parking Orbit: $h_p = 400 \text{ km}$ (Circular)
    \item Target Hyperbolic Excess Velocity: $v_\infty = 3.5 \text{ km/s}$
    \item Edge of Earth's SOI: $r_{SOI} \approx 925,000 \text{ km}$
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Part A: Analytical Calculation}
Using the Vis-Viva equation and the patched conic approximation:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Calculate the specific energy ($C_3$) of the required departure hyperbola. ($C_3 = v_\infty^2$).
    \item Calculate the velocity of the spacecraft in its circular parking orbit ($v_c$).
    \item Calculate the required burnout velocity at the periapsis of the departure hyperbola ($v_p$).
    \item Calculate the required prograde engine burn ($\Delta V_{depart}$).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Part B: GMAT Simulation \& Differential Correction}
In GMAT, we do not target a velocity at infinity; instead, we target the $C_3$ orbital energy directly.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Resources Setup:} Initialize the spacecraft in the 400 km circular orbit. Create an `ImpulsiveBurn` (Prograde/VNB). 
    \item \textbf{Mission Sequence:}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item \texttt{Propagate} the spacecraft to `Sat.Earth.Periapsis` to maximize the Oberth effect.
            \item Create a \texttt{Target} sequence.
            \item \texttt{Vary} the Prograde velocity (`Element1`). Use your analytical $\Delta V_{depart}$ as the initial guess.
            \item \texttt{Maneuver} the spacecraft.
            \item \texttt{Propagate} the spacecraft out to the edge of the Sphere of Influence (`Sat.Earth.RMAG = 925000`).
            \item \texttt{Achieve} the target orbital energy (`Sat.Earth.C3Energy = 12.25`). Set the tolerance to $0.001$.
        \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Deliverable:} Compare your analytical $\Delta V$ with the GMAT DC output. Take a screenshot of the 3D graphics window showing the hyperbolic escape trajectory leaving the Earth.
\end{enumerate}
