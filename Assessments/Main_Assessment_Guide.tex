\section*{Course Assessment Philosophy}
The evaluation strategy for Astrodynamics 2026-1 is designed to mirror the demands of professional aerospace systems engineering. Mathematical competency is expected, but the true measure of your success will be your ability to justify your design engineering choices. 

This course operates on a \textbf{"Trust but Verify"} methodology. You are permitted to use textbooks, code snippets, and AI tools to assist in your computational workflows. However, automated solutions are entirely insufficient on their own. For every major assessment, you must provide \textbf{Chief Architect's Memos (CAMs)}—explicit, line-by-line physical and mathematical justifications proving that you understand exactly what your tools are doing. Design justification is prioritized over AI-generated outputs.

\section*{Detailed Deliverable Expectations}

\subsection*{1. Exam No. 1: Analytical Astrodynamics (20\%)}
This 24-hour take-home exam evaluates your mastery of the fundamental physics governing orbital mechanics. You will use equations of motion under $J_2$ perturbations and calculate $\Delta V$ budgets for Patched Conic trajectories. Final numerical answers without supporting analytical frameworks will receive zero credit.

\textbf{Proposed Date: $7^{th}$ of May to be delivered $9^{th}$ of May at 11:59 pm}

\subsection*{2. Computational Laboratories (30\%)}
There will be three labs distributed throughout the end of the semester. For each lab, you will generate an analytical baseline using Python or GMAT as the high-fidelity numerical simulator. Your grade depends heavily on the "Verification \& Discrepancy Analysis" section, where you must mathematically explain any deviations between your code and GMAT's integrators. The first workshop is already included in this assessment.

\subsection*{3. Exam No. 2: Applied GMAT (20\%)}
Unlike Exam 1, this is a purely computational-based exam in a class space. You will be provided with mission parameters (e.g., a low-thrust Earth escape or a Martian B-Plane targeting sequence) and must submit functional GMAT \texttt{.script} files.

\textbf{Proposed Date: $27^{th}$ of May}


\subsection*{4. Final Project: End-to-End Mission Architecture (30\%)}
Working in small teams (2-3 students), you will act as the mission design bureau for a complete interplanetary mission. This capstone requires a formal Concept of Operations (ConOps), an architectural trade study comparing propulsion systems (Impulsive vs. Continuous), and finalized numerical trajectories. 

\textbf{Proposed Date: $11^{th}$ of June}
